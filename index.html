The Method

The core of the Zettelkasten method can be broken down into three steps, or connected practices:

  1. Take fleeting notes as you go through the day. These are for immediate, short-term storage of thoughts and ideas without worrying about whether they're particularly worth dedicating space in your Zettelkasten to. Ideas are captured here to be sifted through and either refined into Zettels or discarded as appropriateâ€”this means there should be as little friction involved in the capturing of fleeting notes. I personally keep a cheap Â£1 notebook handy to scribble in whenever the need strikes.
  2. Take literature notes as you consume content. Building a robust reference system helps tremendously with managing bibliographical data in academic work, but it's also useful for personal knowledge-work outside of academia, as keeping track of the sources that lead to our own ideas in the first place helps make us better and more rigorous thinkers.
  3. Craft permanent notes, the actual meat of the Zettelkasten system. These are atomic ideas that link to and from other notes. By atomic, what is meant is that a single Zettel should not present multiple distinct ideas that could be better split into two or more smaller Zettels. The power of each note is not in how much information it holds, but in its interrelationship with other related notes via links to and from them.

Together these three practices capture the essence of the system. Many people implement this system the same way Luhmann himself did: with boxes of index cards. I personally enjoy leveraging all the goodies the digital world brings us and use a smartnotes app for my Zettelkasten. Digital notes don't have to be small enough to fit on a single index card, can support massive amounts of bi-directional linking without becoming cluttered, and don't require arbitrary indexing numbers to reference one another.

Benefits

What are some of the benefits of building a 'second brain'?

For a start, traditional note-taking is linearâ€”notes are generally organised chronologically, in the order they were written. Even if they are organised in a folder, we tend at best group notes by related topics, which is itself not ideal as it forces a top-down, hierarchical structure to our notes. In contrast, the Zettelkasten is a non-hierarchical, bottom-up system. Notes are not organised, they are simply created and then linked together over time. In a digital system, notes can be linked to long before even being created, and many people (myself included) use this as a way to allow topics to organically emerge. If certain notes are attracting a conspicuous amounts of backlinks, that is often a good sign they represent emergent topics, and could be promoted to become Maps of Content, or 'MoCs' for short.

As a consequence of its linearity, the conventional practice of writing things down in notebooks creates a body of notes which becomes harder to meaningfully search as it increases in size. We don't write things down to remember them, but to forget them. The retreievability of things when we need them is therefore one of the most important metrics to assess a note-taking system by, and it is here that the Zettelkasten method excels. Notes become more deeply interlinked over time as more connections between them are realised, and the system becomes more useful as it grows, rather than less.

Both Luhmann and Ahrens insist upon the importance of manually writing out brief summaries of the most important points of things we read or otherwise consume, so we are forced to translate ideas into our own words. Adopting this practice acts as a sort of Feynman technique in miniature, embedded within the Zettelkasten method. This step is what makes literature notes not just a useful store of bibliographical data, but a tool to help us integrate the ideas we learn and make them our own.

Graphs

Viewing the organisation of your notes graphically is one of the most satisfying features offered by digital smartnotes applications. Aside from offering insight into emerging topics by making it visually clear which notes are attracting conspicuous numbers of links, they also look really damn cool.

As you can see from this graph of the inside of my vault, there is an enormously dense central cluster, with a sparser ring of notes around the outside. That central cluster contains all the notes from a musical project I worked on last autumn, which happened to deal with a vast amount of highly interlinked data. This visualisation was incredibly helpful when organising things, and seeing where connections were broadly coming from and going to without having to look at individual pages while doing mental arithmetic.

Obsidian also has powerful filtering and search options (including support for regular expressions!), so you can easily hide the things you don't want to see and focus on just the immediately salient connections between notes you're interested in.
